Executive answer

What the combined evidence says

The headline changes when you switch from counts to rates, from all ages to an age group, or from one cancer to another. These are starting points, not the end of the analysis.

01
Modelled

Lung cancer leads the global burden

In 2024, IARC estimates 2.64 million new lung cancers and 1.86 million lung-cancer deaths worldwide.

02
Projected

Population ageing drives a steep count increase

All cancers excluding non-melanoma skin cancer are projected to rise from 19.50 million cases in 2024 to 32.10 million in 2050: +64.6% if 2024 rates remain constant.

03
Modelled

Age profiles are cancer-specific

Most common cancers rise sharply at older ages, while testicular, cervical, thyroid and some blood cancers have distinctly different age patterns.

04
Observed

Country comparison needs like-for-like metrics

Raw counts describe service volume; age-standardised rates are better for comparing populations with different age structures.

Method & limitations

Honest comparisons before attractive charts

  1. Incidence and mortality are different outcomes and are never silently merged.
  2. Observed, modelled and projected values remain visibly distinct.
  3. Age-specific, crude and age-standardised rates answer different questions.
  4. Cancer definitions are mapped to ICD codes and source-defined aggregates are flagged.
  5. Missing national exports are shown as gaps, never filled with invented estimates.
SCOPE & HONESTY

Not survival data. Incidence and mortality cannot be turned into an individual prognosis or a health-system ranking from this dataset alone.

Not causal. Trends can reflect prevention, exposure, screening, diagnosis, coding, population ageing and treatment together.
